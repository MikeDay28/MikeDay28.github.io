Role: Mechatronics member - MechaSci subteam

Brief description: Design and build mechanical subsystems and assemblies to implement autonomous science payload analysis on board the rover.

Skills Summary
- P&ID and flow system design, prototyping, and testing
- Solidworks, 3D printing, machining
- Instrumentation and electro-mechanical integration

Project context: The design team competition requirements include a "science task" where teams must use their rover to analyse dirt samples at different sites for signs of life. The rover must independently collect the dirt sample, properly process them, and perform an on-board analysis for signs of life, then report this information back to the operator. It is the responsibility of the newly formed MechaSci subteam to develop the full system for this science task.

My contributions:
My work on the MechaSci subteam of UBC Rover has largely involved a wide range of large and small scale system design and integration, working to develop different aspects of the project in parallel to meet bulk requirements of the subteam to integrate with the rest of the rover. This has mainly been in the form of designing the flow network of our science sample, the most critical system of the science task, as well as other mechanical systems to properly process and transport samples for analysis. These are described below.
I have also taken on a senior role within the newly formed subteam to assist new members with their individual tasks, providing insight on mechanical design and ideas to ensure these subsystems can integrate with each other, as well as ensuring a solid foundation for future development of the team and project.

Flow distribution and sample processing project:
I took on the flow distribution and sample processing task as one of my first projects on the team. It involves the design and testing of the P&ID system to treat and filter the site samples. The latest version of the system P&ID is below:

Click to enlarge

The P&ID also features electrical components so as to show the full integrated system to be more intuitive for other subteams we collaborate with. The whole system is referred to as the "science module" which is a standalone box containing the whole system that can be connected and disconnected from the back of the rover.

1. The system begins with an input of the raw silt sample into the 'agitator chamber' where it gets mixed with the clean 'rinsing solution'.

2. In the agitator chamber, the silt particles are dissolved into solution where the agitator blade mixes the solution and is intended to break apart proteins through high shear forces.

3. The solution then feeds through various valves before reaching the first optical flow sensor. This is a feedback device to allow us to check for any blockages in the system in case of sediment build up during operation.

4. The peristaltic pump drives the solution through the flow network at a modest rate of about 60ml/min, chosen to minimize the possibility of cross contamination. We have also integrated an H-bridge to allow us to swap the polarity of the pump motor, meaning we can run the pump in reverse. This serves two purposes, to unblock any part of the system and to run a clean rinsing solution through the system when we want to change sites. This further ensures we avoid any cross-contamination between sites.

5. The system then reaches the filter subassembly which will be secured inside the rover chassis, with manual valves to allow easy connection/disconnection. Here, the flow can split into one of the two filter streams depending on which site the sample is from, controlled by a three way solenoid valve. This is another caution for cross-contamination as cleaning the filters during operation is impractical.

6. The solution then flows through another optical flow sensor to monitor for blockages, then into the carousel chamber where sample analysis takes place. The solution drops into a cuvette held in by the carousel, which then spins around to be shown to the spectrophotometer to analyse for life. The carousel chamber must be as dark as possible to avoid light interference with the spectrophotometer.
- Light Source 1 shines a beam of light through the cuvette sample. The specific particles in the sample distort the light in a unique way. This gets detected by the spectrophotometer and Raspberry PI camera which tells us if signs of life are present in the soil. - Light source 2 shines into the carousel chamber, and along with the USB camera we can determine the fluid level of each cuvette. The carousel then rotates to a new cuvette or waste solution index to perform the next actions.
7. After analysis at the first site is complete, the system undergoes a flushing operation to rinse the fittings and tubing to avoid cross-contamination. The flush leads into the waste solution container.
